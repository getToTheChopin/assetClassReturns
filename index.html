Historical Investment Returns by Asset Class

 

This resource shows historical investment returns for a broad set of investment "asset classes" -- stocks, bonds, and beyond.

We'll dig into questions such as:

  • What were the best (and worst) investments of 2020?
  • Is any asset class consistently outperforming the rest?
  • How do different asset classes compare in terms of long-term averages, minimum / maximum returns, and volatility?
  • What is the correlation level between assets? Do bonds perform well when stocks go down (and vice versa)?

This analysis is all about understanding how different investments have performed in the past, so that you can chart out an informed path ahead.

Armed with a good sense of how your investments might perform in the future, you can think through major financial plans or decisions -- whether that's renting versus buying your home, forecasting your retirement age, or just getting a general grip on where your financial trajectory is headed.

 

A Universe of Investment Options

For those who've searched for historical investment returns, you've likely come across data based on the S&P 500 -- an index that tracks the performance of the 500 largest publicly-traded companies in the U.S.

While the S&P 500 index covers the companies that feature prominently in American business news (Apple, Amazon, Microsoft, Google, JP Morgan, and the like), there's a whole wide world out there that isn't being covered.

If you wanted to invest in technology companies in China, bonds in Europe, gold, or real estate, you won't find those options in the S&P 500.

Broadly speaking, the 'universe' of investment options can be broken down by:

  • Underlying asset type -- whether that's stocks, bonds, or other assets such as real estate, commodities, etc.
  • Country -- developed countries (such as the U.S., Canada, Germany) versus emerging markets (such as China, India, Brazil)
  • Risk level -- stocks of large mature companies versus small high-growth firms, government bonds versus high-yield / high-risk corporate bonds

Paul Simon sang about the "50 ways to leave your lover", but surely he forgot about the 100+ ways to build a portfolio...

For each of the 12 asset classes, we'll be working with the annual returns from 1985 to 2020 -- a total period of 36 years. Note that all returns in this analysis are the "real total return" values -- meaning that they are adjusted to take into account inflation, and the re-investment of dividends.

There's quite a bit of data to process, and showing the raw numbers in a table isn't super helpful for digesting the information.

So without further ado, let's get started on slicing, dicing, and prettying up the data.

Correlations Between Asset Class Investment Returns

The chart below shows the correlation level between each pair of asset classes.

The values shown are correlation coefficients (Pearson's R values) that measure the strength of the relationship between each pair of asset classes. A value of 1 indicates a strong positive relationship; a value of -1 indicates a strong negative relationship; a value of 0 indicates no relationship between the two asset clsses.

A few takeaways here:

  • All categories of stocks have fairly high correlation with each other (values close to 1)
  • Bonds have minimal correlation with stocks (values closer to 0)
  • Gold has a slight negative correlation with some of the other assets

Final Thoughts, Notes & Sources

And that's a wrap.

After sifting through a heap of data, my key takeaways are:

  • It's hard (perhaps impossible) to predict which asset class will be the best performer in the future. There doesn't seem to be a rhyme or reason to the annual rankings
  • Investment returns are far more reliable if you have a long time horizon. The likelihood of losing money is much greater if you're investing over a 1-year period rather than a 5, 10, or 20-year period
  • Diversification has clear benefits. Allocating a portion of your portfolio towards bonds will cushion your losses in years when stocks tumble.

All in all, it's my firm belief that the optimal investing strategy comes down a few simple steps -- invest in a diversified passive portfolio, stay invested in the long-term, and re-balance when necessary.
